INVASIVE SPECIES IN ALBERTA WATERS
================================================================================

PRUSSIAN CARP (Carassius gibelio)
================================================================================

STATUS: Prohibited invasive species in Alberta

Prussian carp are one of the most serious threats to Alberta's aquatic ecosystems.
These invasive fish compete with native species for food and habitat, and their
unique reproductive strategy allows them to rapidly colonize new waters.

IDENTIFICATION
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Distinguishing Features:
• Silvery-brown to brassy coloration
• Deep-bodied, laterally compressed
• 29-32 lateral line scales (COUNT THE SCALES - this is key!)
• Concave (curved inward) dorsal fin edge
• Serrated first dorsal ray - feels rough like a saw when you run your finger down it
• Usually 12-18 cm (5-7 inches), can reach 40 cm
• No barbels (whiskers) on mouth

CONFUSING SIMILAR SPECIES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Prussian Carp vs. Goldfish:
• Goldfish (Carassius auratus) are also invasive and prohibited
• Goldfish typically have orange, red, or mottled coloration
• Goldfish also have serrated dorsal ray and similar scale count
• Both species are prohibited - if in doubt, you must still kill them
• Many Prussian carp are actually feral goldfish that have reverted to wild coloration

Prussian Carp vs. Native Suckers (White Sucker, Longnose Sucker):
• Suckers have downward-facing mouths designed for bottom feeding
• Suckers have 55+ lateral line scales (nearly double)
• Suckers have smooth dorsal rays, NOT serrated
• Sucker bodies are more cylindrical, less deep-bodied
• Suckers are NATIVE and protected - proper ID is critical

When in Doubt:
If you catch a silvery fish and aren't certain:
1. Count the lateral line scales (the row along the side)
2. Feel the first ray of the dorsal fin - is it serrated/rough?
3. Count: 29-32 scales + serrated ray = Prussian carp (KILL IT)
4. Count: 55+ scales + smooth ray = native sucker (RELEASE IT)

DISTRIBUTION IN ALBERTA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Current Known Presence:
• Bow River drainage (Calgary area and downstream)
• Red Deer River drainage
• Oldman River drainage
• South Saskatchewan River system
• Various stocked trout ponds (from illegal introductions)

How They Spread:
• Illegal use as live bait
• Illegal aquarium releases
• Illegal stocking by uninformed anglers
• Natural movement through connected waterways
• Flooding events

High-Risk Waters:
Any water connected to the Bow, Red Deer, Oldman, or South Saskatchewan systems
should be considered at risk. They have been found in urban ponds, irrigation
canals, and prairie reservoirs.

REPRODUCTION - WHY THEY'RE SO DANGEROUS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Gynogenesis (Asexual Cloning):
Prussian carp use a rare reproductive strategy called gynogenesis:
• Females produce eggs that are genetically identical clones
• Eggs require sperm from OTHER fish species to activate development
• The sperm DNA is NOT incorporated - offspring are 100% clones of mother
• Males are rare or absent in many populations
• One female can establish an entire population by herself

Why This Matters:
• Populations can explode rapidly without need for males
• All offspring are fertile females that can reproduce
• They can hijack native fish reproduction to make more Prussian carp
• Control efforts must be extremely thorough - even one survivor can restart the population

Reproduction Season:
• Spawn in spring/early summer when water temperatures reach 15-20°C
• Females can produce 100,000+ eggs per year
• Multiple spawning events per season possible

REGULATIONS - MANDATORY KILL
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Legal Requirements When You Catch Prussian Carp:
✓ YOU MUST KILL any Prussian carp you catch
✓ DO NOT release back into the water
✓ Dispose of carcass in garbage or bury on land away from water

Possession Rules:
• ILLEGAL to possess live Prussian carp beyond 5 meters from the shoreline
• ILLEGAL to transport live Prussian carp
• ILLEGAL to use as live bait or release into any water
• Dead Prussian carp may be possessed for disposal

How to Humanely Kill:
1. Severe blow to the head, OR
2. Severe gill cut and bleeding out, OR
3. Freezing (if keeping for identification)
4. Do NOT just leave on shore to die slowly

Penalties:
• Fines up to $100,000 for illegal possession or release
• Provincial court charges for violations
• Additional penalties may apply under federal Fisheries Act

Zero Tolerance:
Alberta takes Prussian carp extremely seriously. There are NO exceptions and
NO limits - all must be killed on capture. Claiming ignorance is not a defense.

REPORTING PRUSSIAN CARP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

If You Catch a Prussian Carp:

1. KILL IT IMMEDIATELY (as described above)

2. REPORT IT:
   • Phone: 1-855-336-2628 (Report A Poacher / Invasive Species line)
   • Online: invasivereport.com
   • Available 24/7

3. PROVIDE INFORMATION:
   • Exact location (GPS coordinates if possible)
   • Date and time
   • Approximate size and number caught
   • Photo if possible (take before disposal)
   • Your contact information

4. KEEP THE SPECIMEN (optional but helpful):
   • Freeze the fish in a plastic bag
   • Label with date and location
   • Alberta Environment may want to examine it
   • Officers can arrange collection

Why Report?
• Helps track spread and establish control measures
• Assists with management decisions
• Identifies new invasion sites early
• Your report could prevent establishment in a new water body

Even if Unsure:
If you THINK you caught a Prussian carp but aren't certain, STILL REPORT IT.
Officers can help with identification and would rather check a native fish
than miss a new Prussian carp population.

ECOLOGICAL IMPACTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Damage to Native Fish:
• Compete with native minnows, young gamefish for food
• Consume zooplankton, insect larvae, plant matter
• Reduce food available for native species
• Can tolerate very low oxygen levels where natives cannot survive

Habitat Degradation:
• Stir up sediments while feeding, increasing turbidity
• Uproot aquatic vegetation
• Degrade spawning habitat for native fish
• Alter nutrient cycling in lakes and ponds

Ecosystem Changes:
• High population densities can fundamentally alter food webs
• Contribute to algae blooms through nutrient cycling
• Once established, extremely difficult or impossible to eradicate
• Economic impacts on fisheries and recreation

Prevention - Your Role:
• NEVER use live baitfish in Alberta (it's illegal)
• NEVER release aquarium fish into the wild
• NEVER move fish between water bodies
• Clean boats, waders, and equipment between waters
• Report any suspicious fish immediately

WHAT IF YOU'RE STILL NOT SURE?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Identification Resources:
• Alberta Environment website: invasive species ID guides
• Local Fish & Wildlife office can assist
• Take clear photos: side view, dorsal fin close-up, scale count area
• When in doubt: call 1-855-336-2628 for phone assistance

Better Safe Than Sorry:
If you catch a fish you can't identify with certainty:
• Don't release it immediately
• Take photos from multiple angles
• Count the scales along the lateral line carefully
• Feel the dorsal fin spine
• Contact authorities before release if uncertain

Remember: Misidentifying and killing a native sucker is unfortunate, but
releasing a Prussian carp could establish a new population that destroys
an entire fishery. When uncertain and unable to confirm ID, authorities
generally recommend retention and reporting for expert identification.

YOUR ROLE IN PROTECTING ALBERTA WATERS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Every angler is a steward of Alberta's fisheries. Prussian carp are a serious
threat that requires vigilance from all water users:

✓ Learn to identify them correctly
✓ Kill every one you catch
✓ Report all catches
✓ Educate other anglers
✓ Never transport fish or water between locations
✓ Support invasive species management efforts

One released Prussian carp can become thousands. Your actions matter.
